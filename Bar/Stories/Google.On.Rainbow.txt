Difference between "onto" and "into". Context: "Most users project onto the interface. A few users project into the interface."
In the context of user interaction with an interface, the difference between "onto" and "into" relates to the depth of immersion and the direction of psychological investment.
1. Projecting ONTO the Interface (Superficial/Defensive)
To project onto something is to treat the interface as a passive surface or mirror for your own existing beliefs and emotions. 
Definition: The user attributes their own internal states (frustration, expectations, biases) to the interface.
Behavior: Most users bring external baggage. If they are stressed, they see the interface as "stubborn" or "difficult." They expect it to conform to patterns they already know (Jakob's Law) rather than learning its unique logic.
Analogy: Like a movie projected onto a screen—the screen doesn't change; it just catches the light of the user's current mood. 
2. Projecting INTO the Interface (Immersive/Identifying)
To project into something implies a more profound psychological connection, often referred in psychology as "projective identification". 
Definition: A few users enter a state of "flow" where the boundary between the self and the tool blurs.
Behavior: These users don't just see a tool; they inhabit the system. They invest their identity and agency into the digital workspace, treating the interface as an extension of their own mind. This is common in "intent-driven" or high-mastery environments where the user's goals and the system's actions feel unified.
Analogy: Like a driver who "feels" the road through the car's wheels—the user has moved past the surface and is operating from within the system's logic.
